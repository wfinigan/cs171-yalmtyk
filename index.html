The Impact of the Legalization of Recreational Marijuana on Boston Drug Incident Reports

How have the type, prevalence, and location of drug violations in Boston changed in response to the legalization of recreational marijuana? Let's explore!

↓

In November of 2016, Massachusetts voters approved a ballot initiative to legalize the drug marijuana for recreational use.

In the months since then, cities across the state -- most notably Boston -- have begun to implement new policies towards regularizing the use of the drug.

Using data from the Boston Police Department, this site visualizes and explains the story of how law enforcement has responded to these changes when it comes to policing the use of Marijuana in Boston. Scroll on to explore the impact of the legalization of marijuana in Massachusetts...

Marijuana Violations by District:

A Close Look at the Policing Strategy of Downtown Boston

The map visualization above shows that the number of arrests for Marijuana (Class D) violations is relatively lower compared to other drug offenses over the timeframe of August 2015 through 2019. One district that we see this is especially prevalent is Downtown Boston or District A-1, which is displayed on the image to the left.

Kenneth Fong, district police captain of A-1 and A-15, spoke on the topic in an interview with the Boston Globe in July 2018. Fong said the department had made it a point to write fewer citations for marijuana and to focus more on educating people on the changes in the law.

“We’re not looking to hit people with fines for smoking pot,” he told the Globe’s Mark Asenault. “The laws are relatively new. People see a headline that says pot is legal now and they may not realize” that public consumption is still prohibited. Here, Fong makes the policing strategy clear: the police are not targeting recreational users, but rather are going after the illegal market.

So... what does that mean?

Decrease in Class D Violations: December 2016
We see a large decrease in number of violations from 2016 to 2017 and 2018. The total number of Class D Drug violations decreased from 677 in 2016 to 316 in 2017 and 344 in 2018. The most significant decrease occurs in and leading up to December 2016, denoted by the line in the graph to the left. As described in the timeline (See Background ), on December 15, 2016, possession of cannabis was decriminalized. In the months leading up to this legalization, it became clear that the public frowned upon Class D violations for possession and favored legalization (click here for more info regarding this statement). Our data demonstrates that the police clearly responded to changing public opinion and the resulting decriminalization by cutting their amount of marijuana arrests.

The Driving Force Behind the Trends: Looking at Enforcement

Boston PD Suggests Not Much Change

On November 20, 2019, one year after the first legal sale of medical marijuana in Massachusetts, the Boston Globe ran an article trying to measure the change that had occurred within those 365 days. The Globe interviewed John Carmichael, the police chief of Walpole, a nearby suburb of Boston, to gauge the impact of Marijuana legalization on drug policing in Massachusetts. “Some of the cruxes of legalization really haven’t panned out for us, public safety-wise,” Carmichael told the Globe. “Reducing the burden on the criminal justice system, reducing arrests and people involved in marijuana crime, protecting youths from diversion or from access to marijuana products, OUI [impaired driving] hasn’t been resolved yet — those issues that really supported the purpose of legalization haven’t panned out yet, and hopefully they will. In five years, 10 years from now, maybe it will.”

. . . And arrests for Class D violations are still in the news . . .

But how can we reconcile our data's demonstration that policing strategy has changed in response to the legalization of marijuana with Carmichael and the news' narrative that not much has changed? The answer can be found by separating violations into possession versus intent to distribute...

Frequency of Marijuana Violations by Type Over Time

Decrease in Class D Violations: December 2016
What do you notice about the relationship over time in possession versus decrease in intent to distribute violations? Here, it is clear that both intent to distribute and possession violations have decreased in absolute terms over the period January 2016-October 2018. However, the number of possession violations has decreased to a much greater degree than intent to distribute violations. Intent to distribute violations have remained relatively constant over the time frame while possession violations have been cut almost in half.

Conclusion: So What?

Looking at the above visualizations we see significant trends in the data regarding law enforcement's response to the legalization of recreational marijuana. These trends exist contrary to the opinion of Walpole police chief John Carmichael discussed earlier.  Class D violations have gone down significantly since the legalization of Marijuana in December of 2016. Looking more closely within Class D violations by offense type, we see that possession of Marjiuana violations have decreased the most while intent to distribute marijuana violations have remained fairly constant. This trend aligns with District A-1 & A-15 police chief Kenneth Fong. Since the changes in the law, cops have focused less on giving violations to people for possession charges and more on educating violators on the changes in the law. Still, they remain going after distributors. It will be interesting to see how these changes continue as more Cannabis Cafes start opening in the Boston area in the coming years.

Marijuana Violations by District

The map visualization shows that the number of arrests for Marijuana (Class D) is relatively lower compared to other drug offenses over our timeframe of August 2015 through 2018. One district that we see this is especially prevalent is Downtown Boston or District A-1, which is displayed on the image to the left.

Kenneth Fong, district police captain of A-1 and A-15, spoke on the topic in an interview with the Boston Globe in July 2018. Fong said the department had made it a point to write fewer citations for marijuana and to focus more on educating people on the changes in the law.

“We’re not looking to hit people with fines for smoking pot,” he told the Globe’s Mark Asenault. “The laws are relatively new. People see a headline that says pot is legal now and they may not realize” public consumption is still prohibited. Here, Fong makes the policing strategy clear: the police are not targeting recreational users, but rather are going after the illegal market.

Not Much Change?

On November 20, 2019, one year after the first legal sale of medical marijuana in Massachusetts, the Boston Globe ran an article trying to measure the change that had occurred within those 365 days. The Globe interviewed John Carmichael, the police chief of Walpole, a nearby suburb of Boston, to gauge the impact of Marijuana legalization on drug policing in Massachusetts. “Some of the cruxes of legalization really haven’t panned out for us, public safety-wise,” Carmichael told the Globe. “Reducing the burden on the criminal justice system, reducing arrests and people involved in marijuana crime, protecting youths from diversion or from access to marijuana products, OUI [impaired driving] hasn’t been resolved yet — those issues that really supported the purpose of legalization haven’t panned out yet, and hopefully they will. In five years, 10 years from now, maybe it will.”

Decrease in Class D Possession Violations

Looking at the clock visualization above we see significant changes over the course of our data. We see a large decrease in number of violations from 2016 to 2017 and 2018. The lightness of 2015 is a bit misleading as that data is from August to December of 2015, less than half the year. The total number of Class D Drug violations decreased from 677 in 2016 to 316 in 2017 and 344 in 2018. The majority of this decrease has occurred at the possession level rather than the intent to distribute level. This is illustrated in the clock visualization as conventional wisdom dictates that the majority of possession charges would occur later in the afternoon/evening while more serious intent to distribute charges could occur at any time.
